About Me

I have a Master's in Computer Science from the University of Pennsylvania. At Penn, I worked on the realms of Cyber Physical Systems (CPS) Security under the supervision of Prof. Insup Lee. I was a member of the PRECISE lab at Penn. I was active in research on Medical Device Security at Penn. My interests lie in areas such as Distributed Systems, Security, Cyber Physical Systems, Autonomous Vehicles and the Internet of Things (IoT). Before joining Penn, I graduated summa cum laude from Washington & Lee University in 2015 with a Bachelor of Science in Computer Science and a Bachelor of Arts in Mathematics. At Washington & Lee, I was a member of the Phi Beta Kappa and Pi Mu Epsilon honor societies, Student Association for International Learning (SAIL), and the Pan Asian Association for International Exchange (PAACE). I was an active member of the W&L Outing Club and I participated in various intramural sports events such as soccer, basketball and volleyball.

My Projects

Research on Cyber Physical Systems

Secure Logger for medical devices and IoT

As a part of a project funded by Intel and NSF, I worked to develop a secure logger for medical devices and IoT using the Intel-Software Guard Extensions (SGX). We used the TLS/SSL encryption and the "enclaves" in SGX to implement a secure logging service for Cyber Physical Systems and the IoT.

Publication: Nguyen, H., Acharya, B., Ivanov, R., ... & Lee, I. (2016, June). Cloud-Based Secure Logger for Medical Devices. In Connected Health: Applications, Systems and Engineering Technologies (CHASE), 2016 IEEE First International Conference on (pp. 89-94). IEEE.

Autonomous Racing

F1/10 Racing Competition

I, along with three other PhD students, developed an F1/10 racing car with the integration of perception and planning for autonomous control. We utilized the NVIDIA Jetson TK1 board with ROS platform in C++. We participated in the First F1/10 Autonomous Racing Competition in ESWEEK 2016 and obtained the first place by finishing a 818 ft lap in 63 seconds. Even though the average speed is only around 10 mph, we were able to get to a top speed of around 25 mph.

The video on the left shows our car completing its first successful lap and it happened five hours before the competition (at 6 am). That is why, we are all very excited.

PyQuadSim

An open-source quadrotor simulator in Python

As an R.E. Lee summer scholar, I worked under Dr. Simon Levy in developing an open-source quadrotor simulator using the Virtual Robot Experimentation Platform (V-REP) in Python. This simulator was designed to work with a number of controllers (R/C transmitter, PS3 controller etc).
